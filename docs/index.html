What is Fiscal Sponsorship? The Basics

Fiscal sponsorship is a powerful, IRS-recognized framework allowing a registered 501(c)(3) public charity (the "Fiscal Sponsor") to offer its legal and tax-exempt status to mission-aligned projects or initiatives (the "Sponsored Project"). This arrangement empowers changemakers—individuals, groups, or new organizations—to solicit tax-deductible donations, apply for grants restricted to nonprofits, and conduct charitable work without the immediate need to establish an independent 501(c)(3) entity.

Think of it as leveraging an established nonprofit's administrative backbone. The sponsor handles key financial and compliance tasks, freeing the project team to focus intensely on program execution and achieving measurable impact.

Why Choose Fiscal Sponsorship? Key Benefits Explored

  • Immediate 501(c)(3) Pathway: Gain the ability to accept tax-deductible gifts and access grant funding *now*, crucial for launching and scaling your mission.
  • Speed & Efficiency: Bypass the often lengthy (6-12+ months), costly, and complex IRS application process for 501(c)(3) status. Ideal for testing innovative ideas or responding quickly to community needs.
  • Reduced Administrative Load: Offload complex back-office tasks like accounting, donation processing, state registrations, and IRS reporting to the sponsor's experienced team and established systems.
  • Enhanced Credibility & Trust: Benefit from the established reputation, governance structure, and oversight of your fiscal sponsor, building confidence with donors and grantors.
  • Laser Focus on Impact: Dedicate your valuable time and resources directly to your charitable programs and beneficiaries, not administrative setup and upkeep.

Understanding Fiscal Sponsorship Models (A & C)

The relationship between a sponsor and project is defined by specific legal models. Cause and Impact excels in supporting the two most common and robust models, ensuring compliance and clarity:

Model A: Direct Project

  • Structure: The project operates as an integral program *of the fiscal sponsor*. It has no separate legal identity.
  • Control & Operations: The sponsor holds complete legal and financial authority. It employs staff, manages all funds directly, assumes liabilities, and owns project assets. Contributions are made *to the sponsor* specifically for the project.
  • Best Use Cases: Perfect for brand-new initiatives needing full support, projects led by individuals, or when direct sponsor management is preferred.

Model C: Pre-Approved Grant Relationship

  • Structure: The project often maintains its own operational identity (sometimes as a separate LLC, B-Corp, or unincorporated association) but works under a formal grant agreement with the sponsor.
  • Control & Operations: Donors contribute *to the fiscal sponsor*. The sponsor then makes grants from these dedicated funds to the project for executing specific, approved charitable activities outlined in the grant agreement. Crucially, the sponsor retains ultimate discretion and control over the funds to ensure IRS compliance. The project manages its daily activities but is accountable to the sponsor for reporting and fund usage.
  • Best Use Cases: Suitable for projects with some existing operational structure, those needing grant eligibility while retaining operational autonomy, or strategic partnerships.

Note: Cause and Impact focuses on Models A and C due to their prevalence and clear compliance pathways, offering a strong alternative to less common or more complex structures like Model B (Independent Contractor) or Model L (LLC Subsidiary).
